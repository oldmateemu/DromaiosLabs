# Warm Outreach Templates — Tier 1 (aged care / community care)

Templates for researched, personal outreach to Tier-1 healthcare leaders. Goal of **every**
message: not a sale, not a demo — one 20-minute conversation to understand their operational
problem (per the company "discovery call framing").

**Rules that make these work**
- Personalise the first line. Generic = ignored. Reference their org, a recent post, a service
  type, a known sector pressure.
- Lead with value or insight, not "can I show you something."
- One ask, easy to say yes to. Short.
- **Guardrail:** no clinical/outcome/TGA claims, no "saves X%/hours," no ClinicBoss feature detail
  or final-brand language, no implied traction. Education and practical problems only.

Channels covered: LinkedIn connection + follow-up, cold-ish email, the free self-assessment as
the opener, partnership/association outreach, and a light follow-up sequence.

---

## A. LinkedIn

### A1 — Connection request (no pitch)
> Hi [First name] — I write about practical safety and compliance in [aged care / community care]
> (de-escalation, documentation, onboarding that actually sticks). Always keen to connect with
> people leading the real work. No pitch — just like learning from operators.

### A2 — Follow-up message after they accept (insight-first)
> Thanks for connecting, [First name].
>
> I keep noticing the same pattern in [aged care]: most incidents don't start as emergencies —
> they start as small moments that escalate because no one's practised the first 30 seconds.
>
> Curious how you see it at [Org]. Is frontline de-escalation something your team gets to
> practise, or is it more "policy in a folder"? Genuinely interested in how you handle it.

### A3 — Soft transition to a conversation
> That's really useful, thanks. This is exactly the kind of operational reality I'm trying to
> understand properly before building anything.
>
> Would you be open to a 20-minute call sometime? No pitch — I'd mostly want to hear where the
> real pressure is for you on safety/compliance/onboarding. I usually come away with something
> useful to share too. Happy to work around your week.

## B. Email

### B1 — Cold email, insight-led (primary)
**Subject:** the first 30 seconds (re: incidents at [Org])

> Hi [First name],
>
> I'll keep this short. I work with [aged-care] teams on the practical side of safety and
> compliance — de-escalation, same-day documentation, onboarding that gets new starters ready
> before they're alone on the floor.
>
> One pattern I keep seeing: most incidents don't begin as emergencies. They begin as small
> moments that escalate because no one's practised what to do in the first 30 seconds. Policies
> don't help in a hallway; rehearsed habits do.
>
> I'm not selling anything in this email. I'd just value 20 minutes to hear how [Org] handles
> that, and what's hardest for your team right now. I tend to leave people with something useful
> either way.
>
> Worth a short call in the next couple of weeks?
>
> [Name]
> Dromaios Labs — practical healthcare education, tools, and operational software

## D. Follow-up sequence (don't quit after one message)

Most replies come from follow-ups, not the first touch. Space them out; stay useful, never naggy.

### D1 — Follow-up #1 (≈4 days later)
> Hi [First name] — floating this back up in case it slipped past. No pressure at all. If now's
> not the time, even a "not right now" is helpful so I'm not chasing.

### D2 — Follow-up #2 (≈1 week later, add value, don't ask)
> Hi [First name] — not chasing a meeting, just thought this might be useful: [link to your best
> practical one-pager or LinkedIn post on documentation/de-escalation]. Happy to send the full
> self-assessment too if it'd help your team leads.

### D3 — Polite close (≈1 week later)
> Hi [First name] — I'll leave it here so I'm not cluttering your inbox. If safety, compliance,
> or onboarding becomes a priority down the track, I'm easy to find. Wishing you and the team
> well.

*(A "close the loop" message often gets the highest reply rate — it removes pressure and
signals you respect their time.)*

---

## E. Discovery-call prep (what to do once they say yes)

Keep the promise: it's a conversation, not a pitch.

- **First 15 min:** understand their world. Ask about the lowest-scoring area if they did the
  self-assessment. "Walk me through what actually happens when…"
- **Listen for the buying signal:** a specific, repeated, painful workflow they own and have
  budget to fix.
- **Last 5 min, only if there's a fit:** propose the smallest useful next step — usually a paid
  workshop or short course (offer-ladder rung 1/2). "A good pilot should be narrow, useful, and
  testable."
- **Always leave them with one useful thing**, even if there's no fit. That's what earns referrals.
- **Never** open ClinicBoss here unless they raise an operational software need *and* trust is
  established — and then only as "a working product stream we're developing."
