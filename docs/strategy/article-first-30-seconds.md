## The first 30 seconds

Ask anyone who's worked a frontline care shift to describe a serious incident, and a strange thing
happens. They almost never describe the incident. They describe the *lead-up* — the small moment
before it became serious. The resident who was a little more confused than usual. The family member
whose frustration had been building for three visits. The staff member who was one short shift away
from their limit.

Most serious incidents in care don't begin as emergencies. They begin as ordinary moments that
escalate. And whether they escalate often comes down to what happens in the first 30 seconds —
before anyone has had time to think, look anything up, or call for help.

That's an uncomfortable idea, because the first 30 seconds is exactly the window our systems are
worst at supporting.

### Why policies don't help in a hallway

Most organisations respond to safety risk by writing it down. There's a policy. There's a procedure.
There may be a thick induction binder and an annual training module. On paper, the organisation is
covered.

But a policy in a folder is no help to someone standing in a hallway watching a situation tip over.
In that moment there's no time to recall a document, no time to weigh options, no time to find the
right page. There's only what the person already has — their instincts, their habits, and whatever
they've actually practised.

This is the gap that quietly drives a lot of incidents: the distance between what's *documented* and
what staff can *do under pressure*. We tend to measure the first and assume it produces the second.
It doesn't.

### Rehearsed, not braver

Here's the thing I keep coming back to: the teams that handle hard moments well aren't braver or
naturally calmer than everyone else. They've just rehearsed.

They've had the unglamorous, low-drama conversation about what to actually say, what to actually do,
and who to actually call when a situation starts to escalate. They've said the words out loud. They've
walked through the realistic scenarios — not the dramatic, once-in-a-career ones, but the Tuesday-
afternoon ones that happen all the time.

Rehearsal turns a policy into a reflex. And reflexes are the only thing fast enough for the first
30 seconds.

You don't need a trainer or a budget to start. A 30-minute team conversation does most of the work:

1. **Pick one realistic scenario** your staff genuinely face — not the worst case, the common case.
2. **Answer three questions out loud:** What do we *say* first? What do we *do* first? Who do we
   *call*?
3. **Write the answers in plain words** and make them the team's shared playbook for that moment.

That's it. You've just converted an abstract risk into a practised habit. Do it for the five or six
situations your team meets most, and you've quietly changed how the next dozen incidents go.

### The second 30 seconds: what gets written down

There's a sequel to the first 30 seconds, and it's almost as important: what happens *after*.

Because the same moment that tests your team's response also tests your documentation. And here too,
the gap between documented and real shows up. The honest test is this: could *any* staff member's
notes — not just your most diligent one's — survive a regulator reading them cold, twelve months
later?

If the answer depends on who happened to be working that day, you don't have a documentation system.
You have a documentation lottery. And lotteries are a bad way to manage risk.

Three habits close that gap, and none of them require new software:

- **Same-day.** A complete record written while the memory is fresh beats a perfect record written
  on Friday from half-memory. Same-day documentation is the cheapest risk reduction available to
  most teams.
- **A consistent shape.** What happened, what was done, what's the follow-up. Every time, by everyone.
  Consistency is what makes a year of records *defensible* rather than just present.
- **The "why."** Staff who understand what good documentation protects them *from* stop treating it
  as box-ticking. The motivation isn't compliance. It's self-protection and team protection.

### Why I'm writing about this

I'm building Dromaios Labs around a belief that's almost boring: healthcare tools and training should
start with the actual work, not with the technology. The first 30 seconds is a perfect example of why.
You can buy a lot of impressive-sounding things for a care organisation, but if they don't change what
a tired staff member does in a hallway on a Tuesday, they haven't changed your safety at all.

So before building anything clever, the work is to understand these real moments properly — how
training actually lands, how documentation actually gets written, where compliance actually breaks
down under a busy shift. One of our product streams, currently referred to as ClinicBoss, is being
developed around exactly these operational and compliance workflows. But I'm deliberately not leading
with a product promise, because the honest order of things is: understand the work, prove you can help
in practical ways, and only then build bigger. In healthcare, credibility is part of the product.

The encouraging part is how much is within reach right now, without buying anything. The first 30
seconds can be rehearsed. Documentation can become a same-day habit. Compliance can be a set of
ownerships rather than a scramble before an audit. None of that is glamorous. All of it is the kind of
practical infrastructure that actually makes care safer and better-run.

If you lead a care team, start with one scenario and one 30-minute conversation this week. You'll
learn more about your real safety than another policy review would ever tell you.
