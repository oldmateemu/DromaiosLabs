## Week 5

### Post 13 — Problem-first (handover gaps)
The most dangerous five minutes in a care shift is the handover.

It's when everything one team knows has to survive the jump to the next team — and most of the
time it survives in someone's memory, a rushed verbal, or a note no one reads.

When something goes wrong after a shift change, the root cause is almost never one person. It's the
gap the information fell through.

You don't fix that with more forms. You fix it by agreeing, as a team, the handful of things that
*must* cross every handover, every time — and making that the non-negotiable shape.

What's the one thing that, if it got lost at handover, would worry you most?

---

### Post 14 — Founder POV
A lot of healthcare technology fails for a reason that has nothing to do with the technology.

It's built far away from the work. It assumes a calm user, a quiet room, and time to think — and
then meets a real shift, where none of those exist.

I'd rather build the other way around: start in the messy reality, understand what the work
actually demands, and let that shape the tool. Slower, less impressive in a demo, far more likely
to survive contact with a Tuesday afternoon.

If you've ever quietly abandoned a system that looked great in the sales pitch — what made you
walk away?

---

### Post 15 — Practical framework (the "one shift" test)
Here's a test for any new process, form, or system before you roll it out:

Watch one real shift try to use it.

Not a workshop. Not a champion who loves it. One ordinary, busy shift. If the process survives
*that*, it'll survive the rollout. If people quietly route around it, you've learned that cheaply,
before you've trained 200 staff on something they'll abandon.

Most failed rollouts weren't bad ideas. They were good ideas that never met the real shift until
it was too late.

What's the last thing you piloted on a single shift before rolling it out?

## Week 6

### Post 16 — Problem-first (compliance reaching the floor)
When a regulation or policy changes, how long until it actually changes what happens on the floor?

For a lot of organisations the honest answer is: it doesn't, really. The update reaches a manager's
inbox, gets filed, maybe gets mentioned in a meeting — and the frontline keeps doing what it always
did.

That gap between "we updated the policy" and "the work actually changed" is where a surprising
number of audit findings live.

The fix isn't another email. It's a clear, owned path from "this changed" to "here's the new
habit, here's who's checking it landed."

How does a change actually reach your frontline today?

### Post 17 — Founder voice (why education first)
People sometimes ask why an organisation aiming at healthcare technology spends so much time on
education and training.

Because training is where you find out what's actually true. You can't sit with a care team,
teach the practical stuff, and hear their real problems without learning exactly where the work
breaks down. That's worth more than any market research.

Education isn't a detour from building good healthcare tools. For us it's the foundation — the part
where you earn the right to build anything bigger.

## Week 7

### Post 19 — Problem-first (key-person risk)
Quick gut-check for any care leader: if your best operator left tomorrow, how much of your safety
and compliance system would leave with them?

If the answer is "a lot," that's not a compliment to them — it's a risk you're carrying.

The strongest teams aren't the ones with a brilliant individual holding it together. They're the
ones where the system would hold even if that person didn't show up. Habits written down. Ownership
shared. Knowledge that lives in the team, not one head.

Resilience is boring on a good day and priceless on a bad one.

Where's your biggest "it all depends on one person" risk?

---

### Post 20 — Practical framework (debrief that changes something)
Most incident debriefs change nothing. They happen, everyone agrees it was unfortunate, and the
same conditions stay in place for the next one.

A debrief that actually works asks three plain questions:
1. What happened, without blame?
2. What, in our system, made that more likely?
3. What's one thing we'll change before next week — and who owns it?

If a debrief doesn't end with one owned change, it wasn't a debrief. It was a discussion.

What's the last real change that came out of one of your debriefs?

### Post 21 — Founder POV (credibility)
In most industries you can fake it for a while. In healthcare, you can't — and you shouldn't be
able to.

The people you're selling to carry real accountability for real safety. They can smell a claim
that's running ahead of its evidence from a long way off. And they're right to.

That's why I keep my language careful, even when it would be easier to hype: working product
stream, not finished platform. Practical infrastructure, not revolution. Earned, not announced.

In healthcare, credibility isn't the marketing. It's the product.

## Week 8

### Post 22 — Problem-first (documentation as protection)
A reframe that changes how staff treat documentation:

It's not paperwork for management. It's the thing that protects *you* — the staff member — when a
decision you made at 3am gets questioned six months later in a calm, well-lit room by someone who
wasn't there.

Good notes are the difference between "here's exactly what I saw and did" and "I think I
remember." One of those holds up. The other doesn't.

When staff understand documentation as self-protection, the box-ticking problem mostly solves
itself.

How do you frame documentation to your team — duty, or protection?
