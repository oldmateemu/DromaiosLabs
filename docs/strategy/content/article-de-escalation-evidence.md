# What the evidence actually says about de-escalation training

Walk into almost any conversation about safety in healthcare, aged care or disability work and someone will say it: *"the staff just need more de-escalation training."* It is the reflexive answer to a frightening, rising problem. It feels obviously right.

So we went looking for the evidence that it works. Here is what we found — and why it changed how we think about building safety education.

## The problem is real, and getting worse

This is not a manufactured crisis. Safe Work Australia's 2024 report on work-related violence found serious workers' compensation claims for assault and exposure to workplace violence rose roughly **56% over five years**. Health care and social assistance is among the highest-claiming industries, and almost **40% of health and community-services workers** report exposure to violent or traumatic events at work — higher than the other industries surveyed. Patients and clients, not colleagues, are the most common source.

Frontline teams are not imagining the danger. They are right to ask for help.

## The uncomfortable finding

Here is the part the sector rarely says out loud: **for the outcome that matters most — actually reducing violent incidents — no de-escalation training model has yet been shown to work in a rigorous evaluation.**

That is not one contrarian's opinion. It is the consistent conclusion of the highest-quality evidence reviews available:

- A systematic review of **38 studies** by Price and colleagues (2015) found training reliably improves staff *knowledge, confidence, and de-escalation performance in simulated scenarios* — but **no strong conclusions could be drawn about its effect on real assaults, injuries or containment**, because the underlying evidence was low quality and conflicting.
- **Cochrane** — the global gold standard for evidence reviews — has looked at this four separate times. The two reviews specific to de-escalation found essentially **no usable randomised evidence at all**: the 2017 review on de-escalation for psychosis-induced aggression found *no trials that met inclusion criteria*; the 2018 review on non-psychosis aggression found a single study that did not even measure the relevant outcomes. Cochrane's 2020 review of education and training to prevent aggression toward healthcare workers (9 studies) concluded that **training may not reduce aggression**, even though it may improve knowledge and attitudes.
- A **RAND Europe** rapid evidence assessment (2019), drawing on 19 reviews and around 800 studies, concluded the evidence is **mixed**: de-escalation training *may help staff manage* aggression but *may not in itself reduce the number of incidents*, and where reductions were seen they were **not always sustained**.
- A 2023 systematic review of forensic psychiatric settings (Brenig et al.) found **five studies, none of them randomised**, and **no relevant impact on the rate of violent incidents** — though staff did report *feeling safer*.

In Australia, Monash University's ISCRR reached a complementary conclusion in 2023: occupational violence in healthcare is a **systemic** problem, and while every health service it studied named de-escalation as a critical component, the real barriers were staffing and resourcing, a culture that normalises and under-reports incidents, and a lack of standardised, practical, tailored training. The message was clear: **this cannot be solved by training alone.**

## What this does *not* mean

It would be easy — and wrong — to read all that as "de-escalation training is pointless." That is not what the evidence says.

What the evidence consistently *does* support:

- **Training reliably builds knowledge, confidence and skill.** Staff demonstrate better technique and report feeling safer and more prepared. That matters — for wellbeing, retention, and how people show up in a crisis.
- **One-off training is not enough.** The signal that does exist points to **sustained, refreshed** training rather than a single annual tick-box course.
- **The most promising leads are systemic, not individual.** Lower-certainty evidence points toward organisation-wide change, person-centred and trauma-informed approaches, and attention to the environment and culture of a workplace — not just an individual's verbal technique. (We say *promising*, not *proven*: even here the evidence is low-certainty.)

In other words: the problem is not that training is worthless. The problem is that the sector has been **selling and buying training as if its effectiveness were settled**, when the honest position is that it mostly improves the things we can measure easily (knowledge, confidence) and has not yet been shown to move the thing we actually care about (incidents).

## Why we are saying this out loud

We are building healthcare safety education at Dromaios. It would be commercially easier to do what much of the market does: assert that our training reduces incidents, attach an impressive-sounding framework, and move on.

We are not going to do that, for two reasons.

First, **healthcare buyers deserve honesty about evidence.** In a sector where credibility is part of the product, overclaiming is a liability, not an advantage.

Second, **the evidence gap is the opportunity.** If the field's weakness is that almost nobody measures whether their training changes real-world outcomes, then the most useful thing we can build is training designed from the start to be *measured* — pre and post, against incident rates, restrictive-practice use, and staff confidence — and to be honest when the answer is "not yet."

That is the commitment we are willing to make publicly: to build de-escalation and safety education that is **evidence-led, sustained rather than one-off, connected to organisational change, and measured for the outcomes that actually matter** — and to publish what we learn, including the parts that don't work.

The sector has had enough confident frameworks. What it is short on is honesty about what the evidence shows, and a serious attempt to add to it.
