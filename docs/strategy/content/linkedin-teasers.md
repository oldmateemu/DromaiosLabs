## Post 1 — The contrarian hook

"The staff just need more de-escalation training."

It's the reflexive answer to a frightening, rising problem in healthcare and care work. Serious workers' compensation claims for workplace violence have climbed sharply, and frontline teams are right to ask for help.

So we did something uncomfortable: we went looking for the evidence that de-escalation training actually works.

What we found surprised us. For the outcome that matters most — actually reducing violent incidents — no de-escalation training model has yet been shown to work in a rigorous evaluation. Not one. The reviews are consistent on this, including four separate Cochrane reviews.

That doesn't mean training is pointless. It reliably builds knowledge, confidence and skill. But the sector has been buying and selling it as if its effectiveness were settled — when it isn't.

I wrote up what the evidence actually says, and why it's changing how we build safety education. It's not the comfortable version, but I think the people doing this work deserve the honest one.

## Post 2 — The honesty / why-we're-saying-it angle

In healthcare, credibility is part of the product.

That's why I keep coming back to a hard question about safety education: if we can't yet prove that de-escalation training reduces real-world incidents — and the evidence says we can't — then how should anyone honestly sell it?

The easy path is to attach an impressive-sounding framework, claim it cuts incidents, and move on. A lot of the market does exactly that.

We're not going to. The honest position is that training reliably improves what's easy to measure — knowledge and confidence — and has not been shown to move the thing we actually care about: incidents and harm.

I find that gap motivating rather than discouraging. If almost nobody measures whether their training changes outcomes, then the most useful thing we can build is education designed from day one to be measured — and to say so plainly when the answer is "not yet."

## Post 3 — The systemic angle

We keep framing violence in care settings as a training problem for individual workers.

The evidence points somewhere harder. Reviews of occupational violence in healthcare — including Australian work — keep landing on the same conclusion: this is a systemic issue. Staffing and resourcing. A culture that normalises and under-reports incidents. A lack of standardised, practical, tailored training. The environment people work in.

A one-day course, however good, can't fix any of that on its own. And where studies do show reductions in violence, the gains often fade without sustained, refreshed effort and wider change.

The more promising leads are organisation-wide: sustained training, person-centred and trauma-informed approaches, and fixing the conditions that make escalation more likely in the first place.

None of this means de-escalation skills don't matter. It means we've been asking them to carry more weight than any training alone can hold.
