## Week 1

### Post 1 — Problem-first (de-escalation)
Most aged-care incidents I hear about didn't start as emergencies.

They started as a small moment — a confused resident, a frustrated family member, a tired staff
member — that escalated because nobody had practised what to do in the first 30 seconds.

You can't policy your way out of that. A document in a folder doesn't help someone standing in a
hallway watching a situation tip over.

What helps is practical, repeated, low-drama practice: what we say, what we do, who we call.

The teams that handle hard moments well aren't braver. They've just rehearsed.

What's one thing your team does in those first 30 seconds that actually works?

### Post 3 — Practical framework (give value away)
A simple test for whether your incident documentation will hold up:

Could any staff member's notes — not just your best one's — survive a regulator reading them cold,
12 months later?

If the answer depends on *who* was working, you don't have a documentation system. You have a
documentation lottery.

Three things that close the gap:
1. Same-day, while memory is fresh.
2. A consistent shape: what happened, what was done, what's the follow-up.
3. Staff who understand *why* it matters, so it isn't box-ticking.

None of that needs new software. It needs a habit everyone shares.

What trips your team up most — time, consistency, or buy-in?

## Week 2

### Post 4 — Problem-first (onboarding)
A quiet driver of turnover in care work: new starters thrown onto the floor before they feel ready.

They hit a hard moment in week one, handle it alone, and decide the job isn't for them. We call it
"not the right fit." Often it was "not the right preparation."

Onboarding isn't paperwork. It's whether someone reaches safe, confident competence *before*
they're the only one in the room.

Get that right and you're not just safer — you keep people.

How long does it take a new starter on your team to feel genuinely ready?

---

### Post 5 — Umbrella / principle
Healthcare innovation should earn trust before it asks for it.

That's the principle Dromaios Labs is being built on: start with practical education and tools,
understand real workflows and safety pressure, and let the longer-term direction grow from that —
not from the loudest claim.

There's no shortage of buzzwords aimed at healthcare leaders. There's a real shortage of practical
systems that hold up under pressure.

That gap is the whole point.

---

### Post 6 — Practical framework
"Everyone's responsible for compliance" is one of the most expensive sentences in care.

Because when everyone owns it, no one does — and the gaps only surface during an audit or after an
incident.

A fix that costs nothing: one named owner per compliance area. Written down. Not a committee, not
"the team." A name.

It changes the question from "did someone handle this?" to "did *you*?" — and that's the question
that actually gets things done.

What's one area in your org that's quietly owned by no one?

## Week 3

### Post 7 — Problem-first (documentation burden)
Care teams aren't short on documentation. They're drowning in it.

The problem usually isn't the amount — it's that the systems were built far away from how the work
actually happens. So good staff spend their energy fighting the admin instead of doing the care.

I keep coming back to one question when I look at any healthcare tool or process:

Does this fit the reality of the shift, or does it add a layer on top of it?

If it's a layer on top, people will quietly route around it — and your "system" becomes optional.

Where does admin get in the way most on your team?

### Post 8 — Founder voice (measured ambition)
People ask why Dromaios Labs isn't leading with a big medtech product.

Because serious healthcare technology shouldn't jump straight to grand claims. The path I'm taking
is deliberately slower: education to understand safety and workforce needs, practical tools to test
what's actually useful, operational software to learn real workflows — and only then anything bigger,
built with the right evidence and governance around it.

In a lot of industries, "move fast" is a virtue. In healthcare, "move carefully and prove it" is
the harder, better discipline.

The slower path is the strategy, not a compromise.

---

### Post 9 — Practical framework (lone/community workers)
If you have community or lone workers, ask one uncomfortable question:

When something goes wrong, does your safety process actually get *used* — or does it just exist in
a manual?

A documented process that staff don't reach for in the moment is a liability dressed up as a control.

Three signs it's real, not just written:
1. Staff can describe it without looking it up.
2. It's been used and refined, not just approved.
3. After an incident, something actually changes.

Documented isn't the same as working. The gap between them is where people get hurt.

## Week 4

### Post 10 — Problem-first (audit stress)
You can usually tell how healthy an organisation's compliance really is by watching the month
before an audit.

If it's calm, compliance is a set of daily habits.
If it's chaos, compliance is a performance staged for visitors.

The chaos version is exhausting, it's fragile, and regulators can smell it.

The shift isn't more binders. It's turning the handful of things that matter into habits the team
does anyway — so the audit is just a normal week someone happens to be watching.

What does the month before an audit look like in your world — calm or scramble?
