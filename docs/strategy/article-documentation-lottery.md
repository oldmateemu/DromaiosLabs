## The documentation lottery

Here's a quiet test that tells you more about an organisation's real risk than most audits do. Pick
an incident from the last year — any one. Now pull the record. Don't read it for what happened; read
it for *who wrote it*. Then ask yourself an honest question: if a different staff member had been on
that shift, would the record be just as complete, just as clear, just as defensible?

For a lot of care organisations, the honest answer is no. The quality of the record depends heavily
on who happened to be holding the pen. Your best people write thorough, clear, time-stamped notes.
Others write less. Some write almost nothing until prompted.

That's not a documentation system. That's a documentation lottery — and lotteries are a poor way to
manage risk.

### Why it stays hidden

The documentation lottery is dangerous precisely because it doesn't look like a problem day to day.
On a normal week, the gaps don't matter. The records that are thin are for incidents that resolved
fine. Nobody goes back to read them. The system appears to work.

It only reveals itself at the worst possible moment: when something serious is being reviewed, often
months later, in a calm and well-lit room, by someone who wasn't there. That's when "I think I
remember what happened" meets "show me the record" — and the gap between your best documenter and
your weakest becomes the difference between a defensible position and an exposed one.

By then it's too late to fix. The record is whatever it was on the day.

### It's not a discipline problem

The instinct is to treat this as a compliance failing — to remind, retrain, and chase. And those
have a place. But mostly, the lottery isn't a discipline problem. It's a design problem.

Think about the conditions under which care documentation actually gets written. The end of a long
shift. A staff member who's tired, behind, and being pulled in three directions. A form built for
completeness, not for speed. No shared sense of what "good enough" actually looks like. Under those
conditions, the variation between people isn't surprising — it's inevitable. You're relying on
individual conscientiousness to overcome a system that makes good documentation hard.

The fix isn't to demand more conscientiousness. It's to make the consistent thing the easy thing.

### Three habits that close the gap

None of these require new software. They're changes to how the team works, and they do most of the
heavy lifting.

**1. Same-day, every day.** The single biggest driver of poor records is time. A complete note
written while the memory is fresh beats a perfect note reconstructed days later from a foggy
recollection — and the foggy version is also the one that reads badly under review. Make same-day
documentation the default expectation, and protect the few minutes it takes. It is the cheapest risk
reduction most teams have available, and it's sitting right there.

**2. A shared shape.** Most variation between staff is really variation in *structure* — what they
think a record should contain. Give the team one simple shape and use it every time: what happened,
what was done, and what the follow-up is. When everyone reaches for the same three-part shape, the
worst notes get dramatically better and the best ones get faster. Consistency is what turns a year of
records into something defensible rather than just present.

**3. The "why."** Staff document well when they understand what good documentation actually protects.
And the honest answer isn't "it keeps management happy." It's that the record protects *them* — the
staff member — when a decision made at 3am is questioned six months later by someone who wasn't in
the room. Reframe documentation from a duty owed upward to a protection owned personally, and the
box-ticking problem starts to dissolve on its own.

### How you'd know it's working

You don't need a dashboard to feel the difference. You'd notice that you could pull any record from
the last year and not wince. That a new staff member's notes look recognisably like an experienced
one's. That the month before an audit is calm, because there's nothing to reconstruct. That when
something does go wrong, the record stands on its own and you're not relying on anyone's memory.

Those are signs of a system, not a lottery. And they come almost entirely from habits and shared
structure — not from spending.

### Where this fits

I write about this because it's exactly the kind of problem I'm building Dromaios Labs around:
unglamorous, operational, and far more consequential than it looks. The documentation lottery isn't a
technology gap. It's a gap between how the work is designed and how it actually happens under
pressure — the same gap I keep finding wherever care teams are stretched.

One of our product streams, currently referred to as ClinicBoss, is being developed around these
operational and compliance workflows — the trail of evidence that a thing was really done. But I'm
deliberately careful about leading with a product, because the honest order of things is to
understand the work first and earn the right to build. And the encouraging part of the documentation
lottery is how much you can fix right now, without buying anything: same-day as the default, one
shared shape, and a team that understands documentation as protection rather than paperwork.

You can't make every staff member your best documenter overnight. But you can stop leaving the
quality of your records to chance — and in care, leaving anything important to chance is a decision,
even when no one decided it on purpose.
