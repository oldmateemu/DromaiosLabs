## Week 9

### Post 25 — Problem-first (the cost of "we've always done it this way")
The most expensive phrase in care isn't in any budget line. It's "we've always done it this way."

It's expensive because it hides risk in plain sight. The handover that's always been a bit rushed.
The documentation that's always been a bit inconsistent. The induction that's always thrown people
in the deep end. None of it looks like a problem — until the day it is one.

You don't need to change everything. You need to look at the one habit everyone's stopped
questioning and ask: if we were designing this today, would we do it this way?

What's one "we've always done it this way" in your team that's overdue a look?

### Post 26 — What we're learning (founder)
A pattern from the conversations I've been having with care leaders this month:

Almost no one's core problem is a lack of policies. They have policies. Their problem is the
distance between the policy and the floor — whether the thing written down is actually what happens
at 2pm on a busy Thursday.

That gap is where I keep finding the real work. Not writing more rules, but closing the space
between the rule and the reality.

It's reshaping how I think about what's actually worth building. More soon.

### Post 27 — Practical framework (the 5-minute audit)
You can get a surprisingly honest read on your team's safety culture in five minutes. Ask any
frontline staff member three questions:

1. If a situation started escalating right now, what would you do first?
2. Where would you write it up, and by when?
3. Who would you tell?

If the answers are fast, specific, and consistent across people, your system is real. If they're
vague or "it depends who you ask," you've found exactly where to focus — before an incident finds
it for you.

Try it on your next shift. The answers are usually clarifying.

## Week 10

### Post 28 — Workshop recap (social proof, careful)
Ran a practical session with a care team this week on handling the first 30 seconds of an
escalating situation.

The part that always lands hardest isn't a technique. It's the realisation, out loud, that the
team had never actually agreed what to *say* and *do* first — everyone had quietly assumed everyone
else knew.

Twenty minutes of building a shared playbook fixed more than any policy document had. Not because
the playbook was clever, but because it was *shared*.

That's the whole game: getting the team to the same page, on paper, out loud, before it matters.

### Post 29 — Problem-first (onboarding under pressure)
When you're short-staffed, onboarding is the first thing to get sacrificed — and it's exactly the
wrong thing to cut.

A rushed new starter is slower, more anxious, more likely to make an error, and more likely to
leave. So cutting their onboarding to cover a roster gap quietly creates the next roster gap.

It feels like you're saving time. You're borrowing it at a brutal interest rate.

Protecting onboarding when you're busiest is one of the hardest, highest-leverage disciplines in
care leadership.

How do you protect onboarding when the roster's already stretched?

## Week 11

### Post 31 — Problem-first (audit as a mirror)
An audit doesn't create your compliance risk. It just shows you the risk you were already carrying.

Which is why the goal isn't to "pass the audit." The goal is to run the kind of operation where the
audit is uneventful because the daily habits were already sound. Pass-the-audit thinking gets you a
frantic month and a fragile result. Habit thinking gets you a calm operation that happens to audit
well.

Same paperwork. Completely different organisation underneath.

Does your team treat audits as a deadline to survive, or a mirror to learn from?

### Post 32 — Practical framework (ownership map)
A 15-minute exercise worth doing with your leadership team:

List your key safety and compliance areas down one side. Next to each, write the *name* of the
person who owns it. Not the team. Not the role. The person.

Two things always happen. You find an area with no name — that's your highest risk. And you find an
area with too many names — that's your second.

Clarity of ownership is cheap, fast, and quietly prevents more incidents than most new systems do.

Want the simple template? Comment "ownership" and I'll share it.

## Week 12

### Post 34 — Problem-first (silence isn't safety)
A quiet month with no incident reports isn't necessarily a safe month. Sometimes it's a month where
people stopped reporting.

When staff believe reporting leads to blame, or that nothing changes anyway, they stop. The numbers
look great right up until something serious can't be hidden.

A healthy reporting culture often looks *worse* on paper at first — more near-misses logged, more
small things surfaced. That's not deterioration. That's trust. And trust is what lets you fix
problems while they're still small.

Do your incident numbers reflect what's happening, or what people feel safe reporting?
