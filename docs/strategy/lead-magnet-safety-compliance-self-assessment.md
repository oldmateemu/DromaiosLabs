## How to score

Rate each statement from 0–4:

- **0** — Not in place / not sure
- **1** — Ad hoc, depends on the individual
- **2** — Partly in place, inconsistent across the team or sites
- **3** — In place and mostly consistent
- **4** — In place, consistent, and we'd be confident showing it to a regulator tomorrow

Max score per section = 20. Max total = 100.

## Section 1 — Frontline safety & de-escalation

1. Every frontline staff member has had practical de-escalation training, not just a policy to read.
2. New starters learn how to handle aggression or distress **before** they're on the floor alone.
3. Staff know exactly what to do, and who to call, when a situation starts to escalate.
4. Lone or community workers have a clear, used (not just documented) safety process.
5. After an incident, the team actually debriefs and something changes.

## Section 2 — Documentation that survives scrutiny

6. Incident documentation is completed the same day, not days later from memory.
7. Records are consistent enough that any staff member's notes would hold up under review.
8. We could pull a complete, legible record for any incident in the last 12 months within minutes.
9. Documentation captures what happened, what was done, and the follow-up — not just an event.
10. Staff understand *why* the documentation matters, so they don't treat it as box-ticking.

## Section 3 — Compliance habits (not just compliance paperwork)

11. Compliance is a set of daily habits, not a scramble before an audit.
12. We know our next key review/accreditation dates and who owns each one.
13. When a requirement changes, there's a clear way it reaches the frontline.
14. We can show evidence of ongoing training, not just a one-off at induction.
15. Responsibility for each compliance area has a named owner, not "everyone/no one."

## Section 4 — Onboarding & workforce capability

16. A new starter reaches safe, confident competence in a predictable timeframe.
17. Onboarding is the same quality regardless of who runs it or which site.
18. We can show what each role must be able to do, and evidence they can do it.
19. Refresher/ongoing training is scheduled, not reactive.
20. Turnover isn't quietly driven by staff feeling unprepared or unsupported.

## Section 5 — Systems & workload reality

21. Our systems fit how the work actually happens, rather than adding admin on top.
22. Information needed at the point of care is easy to find, not buried.
23. Leaders get early signals about risk, instead of finding out after an incident.
24. The team isn't relying on a few key people holding it all in their heads.
25. If a top performer left tomorrow, the safety/compliance system would still hold.

## Your result

Add up all five sections for a score out of 100.

### 0–40 — At risk
The system is leaning on individuals and goodwill. One departure or one bad week could expose
real gaps. **First move:** pick the single lowest section and put a basic, consistent process in
place this month. Don't try to fix everything at once.

### 41–70 — Functional but fragile
The basics exist but they're inconsistent across people or sites, which is exactly where incidents
and audit findings hide. **First move:** standardise your weakest two sections so quality doesn't
depend on who's working that day.

### 71–100 — Strong, worth protecting
You've built real habits. The risk now is drift as you grow or as staff change. **First move:**
lock in what's working with light, regular refreshers and clear ownership so it survives turnover.

## Three practical first moves (everyone gets these)

1. **Make documentation same-day.** The single cheapest risk reduction is records written while
   memory is fresh. Pick one shift this week and try it.
2. **Give de-escalation a 30-minute team refresher.** Not a policy reread — a short, practical
   "what we actually do when it escalates" conversation.
3. **Name an owner for every compliance area.** "Everyone's responsible" means no one is. One name
   per area, written down.
