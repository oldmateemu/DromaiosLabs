# Email Nurture Sequence — "Practical Safety & Compliance" (first 6 emails)

Sent to everyone who downloads the self-assessment. Goal: stay useful, build trust, and earn the
20-minute conversation — without pitching hard. Roughly one email every 4–5 days, then drop to
fortnightly (the ongoing list email).

**Voice:** founder-led, plain, generous. Every email gives something usable on its own.
**Guardrail:** no clinical/outcome/TGA claims, no unmeasured savings, no ClinicBoss feature detail
or final-brand language. Education and real operational problems only.

Placeholders: `[First name]`, `[link]`, `[booking link]`.

## Email 2 — The first 30 seconds (de-escalation) (Day 4)
**Subject:** most incidents are decided in 30 seconds

> Hi [First name],
>
> Here's a pattern worth sitting with: most serious incidents in care don't start as emergencies.
> They start as a small moment — confusion, frustration, fatigue — that escalates because no one
> had practised what to do in the first 30 seconds.
>
> A policy in a folder doesn't help someone standing in a hallway watching a situation tip over.
> Rehearsed habits do.
>
> Try this with your team this week — it takes 30 minutes, no trainer required:
> 1. Pick one realistic scenario your staff actually face.
> 2. Ask: "What do we *say* first? What do we *do* first? Who do we *call*?"
> 3. Write the answers down in plain words. That's your team's shared playbook for that moment.
>
> Teams that handle hard moments well aren't braver. They've just rehearsed.
>
> — [Name]

## Email 3 — Documentation that survives scrutiny (Day 9)
**Subject:** the documentation lottery

> Hi [First name],
>
> Quick test: could *any* staff member's incident notes — not just your best one's — survive a
> regulator reading them cold, 12 months later?
>
> If the answer depends on who was working, you don't have a documentation system. You have a
> documentation lottery.
>
> Three things close the gap, none of which need new software:
> 1. **Same-day.** Records written while memory is fresh beat perfect records written Friday.
> 2. **A consistent shape:** what happened → what was done → what's the follow-up.
> 3. **The "why."** Staff who understand what documentation protects them *from* stop treating it
>    as box-ticking.
>
> Pick one shift this week and try same-day documentation as a default. It's the cheapest risk
> reduction there is.
>
> — [Name]
>
> P.S. If documentation was your lowest section in the self-assessment, you're far from alone —
> it's the most common one I see.

## Email 4 — Compliance as habits, not binders (Day 14)
**Subject:** the month before an audit tells you everything

> Hi [First name],
>
> You can read an organisation's real compliance health by watching the month before an audit.
>
> Calm? Compliance is a set of daily habits.
> Chaos? Compliance is a performance staged for visitors — fragile, exhausting, and regulators can
> smell it.
>
> One move that quietly shifts you from chaos toward calm: **one named owner per compliance area.**
> Not "the team," not a committee — a name, written down. It changes the question from "did someone
> handle this?" to "did *you*?" and that's the question that actually gets things done.
>
> Where in your org is something important quietly owned by no one? That's usually the first place
> to put a name.
>
> — [Name]

## Email 5 — Onboarding & keeping people (Day 19)
**Subject:** why good new starters quietly quit

> Hi [First name],
>
> A quiet driver of turnover: new starters put on the floor before they feel ready. They hit a hard
> moment in week one, handle it alone, and decide the job isn't for them. We call it "not the right
> fit." Often it was "not the right preparation."
>
> Two questions worth asking about your onboarding:
> 1. Does a new starter reach safe, confident competence in a *predictable* timeframe — or does it
>    depend entirely on who trains them?
> 2. Can you show what each role must be able to *do*, and evidence they can do it?
>
> Getting this right isn't just safer. It's how you keep the good people you worked hard to hire.
>
> — [Name]

## After the sequence — ongoing list email (fortnightly)
Keep it to one structure so it's easy to write and easy to read:
1. **One real problem** (a pattern you're seeing).
2. **One practical tip** they can use this fortnight.
3. **One soft CTA** (a resource, a post, occasionally the booking link).

Repurpose each one into 2–3 LinkedIn posts. Reply-driven: always invite a reply, and write the
next email from what people tell you.

## Notes
- **Segment swap:** built aged-care first; change examples for community/disability care or clinic
  groups to run a parallel sequence.
- **Don't over-automate the reply prompts** — replies are your warmest signal. Watch who engages and
  move them to personal outreach (see `outreach-templates-tier1.md`).
- **Guardrail check** every send: nothing implying clinical outcomes, savings, traction, or
  ClinicBoss specifics.
